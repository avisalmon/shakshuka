⚔️ The Great Debates

Every shakshuka lover has opinions. Strong ones. Pick your side.

הוויכוחים הגדולים של השקשוקה

Onion or No Onion?

בצל או בלי בצל?

Only 10 out of 21 recipes in our research use onion — a major stylistic divide.

🧅 Team Onion
🚫 No Onion
💡 The 'best recipe' source declares explicitly: לא משתמשים בבצל. Eyal Shani and Orly & Shay-Lee also skip it. Counter-position: Sarit Atar, Rotem Liberzon, and Anat Label build on onion for sweetness and depth.
🍳

Cheese: Yes or Heresy?

גבינה: כן או חילול הקודש?

10 out of 22 recipes in our research include cheese — Bulgarian, feta, mozzarella, even vegan.

🧀 Cheese Please
✋ Pure & Classic
💡 Cheese appears in traditional ANU Museum recipes, commercial brands (Mutti), and professional chefs alike. It's added after sauce reduction so it doesn't 'break' the sauce.
🍳

Lid On or Off?

מכסה או בלי?

This produces genuinely different results — it's physics, not opinion.

🫕 Lid On
🍳 Lid Off
💡 Lid on = steam cooks from above → wetter, more uniform egg. Lid off = drier texture, avoids the 'white film' (קרום לבן) on yolk. Racheli Krut warns against lid during egg stage; Eyal Shani explicitly says no lid.
🍳

Fresh or Canned Tomatoes?

עגבניות טריות או משומרות?

Professional consensus: seasonality matters. But in Israel, shakshuka became 'tomatoes year-round.'

🍅 Fresh Only
🥫 Canned is Fine
💡 Rafi Cohen explicitly warns against unripe tomatoes. The Italian approach: fresh only when ripe and in-season (summer), canned in winter — כמו שהאיטלקים עושים.
🍳

Peel Tomatoes or Not?

לקלף את העגבניות?

Sarit Atar addresses the 'peeling myth' — 'I don't peel.'

🔪 Always Peel
💪 Skins Stay
💡 Blog commenters defend not peeling for nutritional value and color. Some chefs insist on blanching and peeling (חליטה+קילוף) for a smoother sauce.
🍳

Wells or Scrambled?

בארות או מעורבב?

Edi Mizrachi's 'Jerusalem Shakshuka' proved the name is flexible.

🕳️ Egg Wells
🥄 Scrambled In
💡 The 'Jerusalem Scrambled Shakshuka' (שקשוקה ירושלמית) from MasterChef is essentially scrambled eggs on a short sauce — proving the name's definition isn't locked to 'egg in a well.'
